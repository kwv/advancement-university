## The Language of AI

Before we go further, let's define the terms:

- **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** â€” computer systems that perform tasks that normally require human intelligence
- **Machine Learning (ML)** â€” a type of AI where systems learn from data instead of being explicitly programmed
- **Narrow AI** â€” AI designed to do one specific task (most AI today)
- **General AI** â€” hypothetical AI that can do *any* intellectual task a human can
- **Superintelligent AI** â€” hypothetical AI that surpasses human intelligence in every area

Note: Ask scouts which of these they think exists today. Only narrow AI is real and widely deployed. General AI and superintelligent AI remain theoretical.

--

## More Key Terms

- **Automation** â€” using technology to perform repetitive tasks without human intervention
- **AI Agents** â€” AI systems that can perceive their environment and take actions to achieve goals
- **Bots** â€” automated programs that perform tasks over the internet
- **Digital Workers** â€” AI or software systems that handle business processes previously done by humans

Note: The distinction between automation and AI is important â€” automation follows fixed rules; AI learns and adapts.

--

## Data and Workflow Terms (1/2)

- **Data** â€” raw facts and figures (numbers, text, images, audio) â€” the fuel that powers AI
- **Database** â€” organized collection of structured data
- **Variables** â€” named storage locations that hold data values
- **Basic Programming** â€” instructions written in code that tell a computer what to do

Note: These are the foundational building blocks â€” data is what AI learns from, programming is how you direct it.

--

## Data and Workflow Terms (2/2)

- **Tasks** â€” individual units of work a system performs
- **Triggers** â€” events or conditions that cause a task to start
- **Workflows** â€” sequences of tasks that accomplish a larger goal
- **Prompt Engineering** â€” the skill of writing effective instructions for AI systems

Note: The data â†’ trigger â†’ workflow â†’ task chain is how almost all automation and AI systems are structured.

## 4d â€” The Turing Test

**What is it?**

Proposed by mathematician **Alan Turing** in 1950.

A human judge has text conversations with:
- Another human
- A machine

If the judge **can't reliably tell which is which**, the machine passes the Turing Test.

**Does passing mean the machine is "intelligent"?**

> That's one of the deepest questions in computer science â€” and it's still debated.

Note: Some philosophers argue the Turing Test only measures the ability to imitate, not true understanding. Ask scouts: "Could ChatGPT pass the Turing Test?"

## 5a â€” What Is a Deepfake?

**Deepfake** â€” AI-generated media (video, audio, or images) that realistically depicts someone saying or doing something they never said or did.

**How it works:**
- AI analyzes thousands of images/videos of a real person
- It learns to map their facial movements, voice patterns, expressions
- It can then generate convincing fake video of that person

--

## 5a â€” How Deepfakes Affect Individuals

## 5b â€” What to Do If You're Affected

**If you or someone you know is targeted by a deepfake:**

**Immediate steps:**
- Do NOT share or engage with the content â€” that spreads it further

## 6a â€” How AI Learns (and Its Limits)

**The AI learning process:**
1. Collect massive amounts of training data
2. Feed it through a model that adjusts based on errors
3. Repeat millions of times until the model performs well
4. Test on data it has never seen before

**Important limitations:**
- AI only knows what's in its training data â€” no common sense beyond that
- It can "hallucinate" â€” generate confident-sounding wrong answers
- It has a knowledge cutoff â€” it doesn't know about recent events
- It can't truly reason â€” it finds patterns, not understanding
- Biased training data produces biased outputs

Note: The hallucination point is critical for teens using AI for homework. "AI said it" is not a reliable citation.

--

## 6b â€” Five Methods to Communicate Effectively with AI

1. **Be specific** â€” vague questions get vague answers
2. **Provide context** â€” tell the AI who you are and what you're trying to accomplish
3. **Give examples** â€” show the format or style you want
4. **Iterate** â€” treat it like a conversation, not a one-shot query
5. **Verify outputs** â€” always check AI answers against reliable sources

Note: Run a live demo if possible â€” show a vague prompt vs. a specific prompt and compare the outputs.
